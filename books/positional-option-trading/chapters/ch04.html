A Defining and Understanding the Variance Premium

The variance premium, also known as the volatility premium, describes the consistent tendency for implied volatility to be higher than the subsequently realized volatility. This concept has deep historical roots, dating back to the early 1900s, and is fundamental for all options traders, as it directly influences profitability, even for those primarily focused on directional trades.

Understanding the variance premium is crucial because it exists across a wide array of markets, including equity indices, bonds, commodities, and currencies. The core insight for traders is that by recognizing and accounting for implied volatility's upward bias, they can employ strategies that sell implied volatility, thereby gaining an inherent advantage from this persistent market phenomenon. The implied variance premium, specifically, quantifies this difference, often leading to profitable strategies such as shorting VIX futures, particularly in contango market structures where futures prices exceed spot prices.

B Market Evidence and Strategic Implications

Empirical data from equity indices consistently demonstrates the presence of a variance premium, with the VIX (representing implied volatility) typically surpassing realized volatility. This trend is observed across major indices like the S&P 500 and NASDAQ, with varying premium levels correlating with overall market volatility. Furthermore, evidence confirms that the variance premium is not confined to equities but extends to commodities, bonds, and currencies, often driven by a systemic demand for insurance against market downturns and other risk factors.

Strategies designed to sell index volatility or short variance swaps have historically shown robust returns, characterized by strong Sharpe ratios. These returns are significantly influenced by factors such as implied correlation and skew. The consistent profitability of these strategies reinforces the idea that being short options has historically been a viable and often profitable approach to trading.

C Reasons for Persistence and Future Outlook

Several interconnected factors contribute to the enduring existence of the variance premium. These include the fundamental demand for insurance by investors seeking downside protection, the presence of 'jump risk' where options hedge against sudden price movements, market restrictions that limit the selling of naked options and thus create upward pressure on implied volatility, and market-maker strategies that involve holding long option positions to manage inventory risks. Additionally, research suggests that the path dependency of returns‚Äîhow returns are realized‚Äîinfluences investor satisfaction, potentially justifying higher option prices.

Despite concerns about the 'Peso Problem,' which questions whether the variance premium merely reflects the possibility of unforeseen, large-scale events, historical data consistently supports its persistence. In conclusion, the consistent and lasting presence of the variance premium indicates that it is a fundamental aspect of options trading. Traders must acknowledge this upward bias in implied volatility, as it underpins the historical and likely future profitability of strategies that involve being short options.
