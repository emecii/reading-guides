Section Breakdown

Deep Dive into Content

A Volatility Positions and the Illusion of 'Repair'

Options provide numerous avenues for expressing views on market movements, especially concerning volatility. However, not all strategies yield equally beneficial results, making it crucial to understand the distinct outcomes of various option positions. This chapter particularly delves into strategies from the perspective of a volatility seller, examining how different structures perform under varying market conditions.

The concept of 'repair' in trading, which suggests turning losing trades into winning ones, can be a misleading notion. Instead, traders are advised to continuously evaluate their current positions based on their most up-to-date forecasts, irrespective of prior performance or emotional attachment to past decisions. This objective approach helps avoid compounding errors and ensures that capital allocation is always aligned with current market expectations.

B Straddles, Strangles, and Simulation Insights

Straddles and strangles are foundational strategies for selling volatility. A short straddle involves selling both a put and a call option at the same strike price, while a short strangle uses different strike prices. The profitability of these strategies is heavily dependent on the interplay between implied volatility, realized volatility, and the returns of the underlying asset.

Simulation results offer critical insights into the profit distributions of these strategies. Short straddles, for instance, may show a higher maximum profit potential but also carry significant downside risk. Short strangles, while potentially yielding smaller, more frequent profits, often expose traders to even higher potential losses due to their wider range of risk. Crucially, poor directional forecasts can lead to substantial losses in both strategies, with strangles typically exhibiting greater downside exposure than straddles.

C Advanced Strategies, Expiration, and Risk Management

More conservative strategies like butterflies and condors are designed to limit downside exposure, with butterflies generally showing higher profits despite increased transaction costs. Delta-hedged positions can minimize directional risk, but this comes at the cost of higher transaction costs and increased operational complexity. Utilizing implied volatility skew and strategically choosing out-of-the-money options can help maximize the capture of the volatility premium.

Expiration selection is another critical factor; short-dated options often exhibit higher variance premiums, making them attractive for sellers. Studies suggest that options closer to expiration tend to capture more profitability. Ultimately, strategy selection should align with individual risk preferences, and while strategies like strangles and condors may boast high win percentages, distinguishing skill from chance remains a challenge. Optimal returns emphasize robust risk management, especially given the highest volatility premium resides in short-dated options.

Butterfly Spread for Limited Risk
A trader uses a butterfly spread (buying one OTM call, selling two ATM calls, buying one OTM call further out) to cap both potential profits and losses. This strategy is conservative, benefiting from the stock staying near the middle strike, but with defined maximum risk.
Complex option spreads like butterflies can be used to limit risk exposure while still profiting from specific volatility or directional forecasts.

Short-Dated Option Premium

Short-dated options often offer the highest variance premium, making them attractive for volatility sellers who manage risk effectively.
Notable Quotes

Key Passages

But just because there are many ways to express an opinion doesn't mean they will all be equally good. The differences are not trivial. Some will be a lot worse than others.

Introducing the idea that option strategies, despite their variety, have vastly different risk/reward profiles.

The loss is already in your account. That money is gone. Forget about the original trade and ask yourself, 'Given what I now know, what position do I want?'.

Advising traders to avoid the 'sunk cost fallacy' and make decisions based on current information, not past losses.

If we can keep plugging along, the profits should eventually take care of themselves.

Suggesting that consistent application of a sound strategy, despite short-term fluctuations, will lead to long-term profitability.

There is no magic in selling strangles, even if they are struck a long way out of the money. If you don't have an edge in volatility, you will lose eventually.

Warning against the misconception that volatility selling is a guaranteed profit, emphasizing the need for an underlying edge.

Risk management should aim to protect expected value rather than minimize risk. The safest position is no position. That will also make no money.

Defining the true purpose of risk management as preserving potential gains, not just avoiding all risk.
