Argument Flow

How the Chapter Builds Its Case

1
The Metaphorical Nature of Models
Sinclair argues that models like Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) are not meant to capture reality perfectly but serve as useful metaphors for practical trading. They allow traders to convert volatile prices into a more stable parameter: implied volatility.
2
The Irrelevance of Underlying Returns
A core tenet of BSM is that the expected return of the underlying asset is irrelevant to an option's instant value. This is maintained through put-call parity and risk-neutral pricing frameworks.
3
Hedging as Volatility Capture
Effective delta hedging is not about predicting price direction but about capturing the difference between implied and realized volatility. This process isolates the volatility premium from market noise.
4
The Volatility Surface Reality
The 'volatility smile' proves that BSM assumptions are technically incorrect across different strikes and maturities. Traders must use the model as a communication tool rather than a literal truth.
