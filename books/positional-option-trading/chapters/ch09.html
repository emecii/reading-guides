A Risk Management and the Kelly Criterion

Risk management is an indispensable aspect of trading; it cannot transform a fundamentally flawed strategy into a profitable one, but it is essential for preventing a good strategy from becoming a loser. It stands as the sole element entirely within a trader's control, underscoring the critical importance of its meticulous application.

The Kelly criterion is a seminal trade-sizing methodology aimed at maximizing the long-term growth rate of capital. Theoretically, it offers superior performance compared to any other sizing strategy. However, traders often deviate from full Kelly betting, prioritizing alternative objectives such as minimizing portfolio volatility, achieving specific financial goals within a defined timeframe, or simply due to personal risk aversion.

B Adapting Kelly for Non-Normal Returns and Uncertainty

For options traders, the practical application of the Kelly criterion necessitates two crucial extensions: accounting for non-normal trade returns and addressing parameter uncertainty. Option outcomes frequently deviate from the ideal normal distributions assumed in simpler models, requiring more sophisticated mathematical techniques to estimate optimal bet sizes, especially when dealing with discrete sets of probabilities.

When dealing with continuous non-normal outcomes, calculations involve maximizing the growth rate of the bankroll based on the assumed return distribution. Adjustments for skewness are particularly vital: positive skewness in returns (potential for large gains) suggests an increase in the optimal investment fraction, while negative skewness (potential for large losses) mandates a decrease. Incomplete knowledge about returns and probabilities (parameter uncertainty) further complicates optimal sizing, requiring cautious adjustments.

C The Debate on Stop-Losses and Practical Sizing

The use of stop-losses in trading is a contentious topic. While they prevent catastrophic losses on individual trades, stop-losses can paradoxically lower long-term returns by prematurely exiting positions that might have recovered and become profitable. Their effectiveness often relies on the assumption of positive autocorrelation in price movements, meaning a move that has already occurred is predictive of a future move in the same directionâ€”an assumption that requires strong justification and is often incorrect.

Ultimately, trading according to a fractional Kelly criterion on a percentage-based subaccount is often suggested as the most practical and robust approach. This method seeks to balance the theoretical maximum growth with a more manageable level of volatility and drawdown risk, making it suitable across various trading criteria and personal preferences.

Full Kelly vs. Fractional Kelly
A trader applies the full Kelly criterion to a strategy with a 60% win rate and 1:1 risk/reward. While theoretically maximizing growth, the resulting trade sizes lead to extreme volatility and large drawdowns. A more practical approach is using 'fractional Kelly' (e.g., 50% of the Kelly amount) to reduce volatility while still achieving strong growth.
The full Kelly criterion can be too aggressive; fractional Kelly offers a better balance of growth and risk management for most traders.

Non-Normal Option Returns
A strategy involving selling out-of-the-money options typically yields many small wins and a few large losses (negative skewness). A trader using the Kelly criterion must adjust their bet size downwards to account for this negative skew, as ignoring it would lead to over-betting and potential ruin.
Non-normal return distributions, especially skewness, must be factored into trade sizing to avoid miscalculating optimal bet sizes.

The Stop-Loss Dilemma
A trader sets a rigid stop-loss on every position. While this prevents large losses, they frequently get stopped out just before the trade reverses and becomes profitable. Over time, these 'small' losses accumulate and reduce overall returns, illustrating the cost of stops.
Stop-losses can hinder long-term returns if they prematurely exit potentially winning trades, especially if based on arbitrary price levels.

Parameter Uncertainty
A trader estimates their strategy's win rate and average payout. However, these parameters are not known with 100% certainty. To account for this 'parameter uncertainty,' they reduce their Kelly bet size, acknowledging that their estimates might be slightly off.
Uncertainty about a strategy's true parameters necessitates a more conservative approach to trade sizing.
