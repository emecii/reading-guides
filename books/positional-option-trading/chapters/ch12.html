Deep Dive into Content

A Adjusting for Interest Rates and Dividends

The Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) model's foundational assumption of a single, constant risk-free interest rate deviates from market reality, where rates vary across bid-ask spreads and yield curves. While large financial institutions manage interest rate risks, independent traders typically forgo hedging due to prohibitive transaction costs. Empirically, errors in interest rate inputs have a minimal impact on option pricing and delta, and even incorporating stochastic interest rates offers negligible improvements in pricing accuracy.

Another core BSM assumption is the absence of dividends. To rectify this, options are priced by subtracting the present value of expected dividends from the underlying stock price. This adjustment is relatively straightforward and can also be applied to situations where stocks become difficult to borrow, reflecting the cost of carrying a short position.

B Addressing Taxes and Underlying Tradability

The BSM model's oversight of varying tax implications among investors complicates its accuracy, as different tax rates can lead to distinct option valuations. While some empirical studies indicate minor price discrepancies attributable to tax effects, these are often insufficient for comprehensive analysis, making a universally tax-adjusted pricing model impractical.

The BSM framework fundamentally relies on the ability to trade and short the underlying asset for effective hedging. In scenarios where the underlying is illiquid or cannot be shorted, the no-arbitrage conditions underpinning BSM break down, leading to potential mispricing. In such cases, traders may resort to a 'real options' approach or utilize imperfectly correlated traded assets for approximate valuations.

C Handling Non-Constant Volatility and BSM's Enduring Relevance

Perhaps the most significant deviation from BSM's assumptions is the reality of non-constant volatility. This leads to phenomena like the 'volatility smile' or 'surface,' where implied volatility varies across different strikes and maturities. Market makers typically address this by using a modified BSM model that incorporates a different volatility for each strike, which they update periodically. While more complex stochastic volatility models exist, they often lead to more variable day-to-day P&L.

Despite its many clearly incorrect assumptions, the BSM model remains an industry standard due to its simplicity, widespread implementation, and the intuitive framework it provides for trader communication. Most of its fundamental assumptions can be corrected through simple, albeit sometimes ad-hoc, adjustments, making it a highly practical and enduring tool for options trading.
