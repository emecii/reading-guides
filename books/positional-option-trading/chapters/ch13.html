A Heuristics for Quick Estimates

The term 'rule of thumb' often carries misinterpretations, frequently rooted in myth rather than historical fact. Nevertheless, simple heuristics can serve as invaluable tools in trading, offering quick estimates and establishing baselines, particularly in situations where comprehensive data analysis is impractical or impossible. These approximations provide a practical way to gain insights without extensive computational effort.

To price options or simulate outcomes, traders often need to estimate mean returns and variance from qualitative information. When fundamental analysts provide a range of possible outcomes rather than precise values, these can be converted into annualized percentages. This conversion commonly employs a triangular distribution approach, allowing for the weighting of estimates based on the analyst's perceived confidence, thus translating qualitative insights into quantitative inputs.

B The Rule of Five and Rule of Three

The Rule of Five is a heuristic asserting that by randomly sampling a population five times, one can be 93% certain that the true population median falls within the range of those five measurements. While the resulting confidence interval may be broad, even such a minimal sample can establish a foundational rate or understanding, making it useful for initial assessments.

The Rule of Three provides a method for estimating the probability of an event that has not yet been observed. It suggests a 95% upper bound on the occurrence rate based on the number of observations. For instance, if an event has not occurred in 30 trials, its likelihood of happening next is estimated at 10%. This rule is most applicable when assuming constant probability and independent observations, but its reliability diminishes in contexts where these assumptions do not hold, such as rapidly changing market conditions.

C Context and Cautions in Application

While these statistical rules of thumb can offer valuable insights, their approximate nature and dependence on context necessitate caution. The accuracy of these heuristics is heavily reliant on the validity of their underlying assumptions, particularly the independence of observations and the constancy of probabilities. Traders must critically evaluate whether these conditions are met in their specific trading scenarios.

Misapplication or an uncritical acceptance of these rules can lead to flawed conclusions and poor trading decisions. Therefore, while useful for providing quick mental models or initial estimates, heuristics should always be used with an awareness of their limitations and as a complement to more rigorous analysis when circumstances permit.

Rule of Three for Tail Risk
A new trading strategy has been tested 100 times without a catastrophic 'blow-up' event. Using the Rule of Three, the trader estimates there's still a 3% upper bound (3/100) on the probability of such an event occurring in the next trial, reminding them of persistent tail risk.
The Rule of Three helps quantify the unobserved risk of rare events, preventing overconfidence from a lack of past occurrences.

Contextual Limitation of Rule of Three
A trader applies the Rule of Three to a stock that hasn't declared bankruptcy in 50 years. However, the company's industry just faced disruptive technological change. The rule's assumption of constant probability is violated, making its estimate for future bankruptcy unreliable.
Heuristics like the Rule of Three are sensitive to their underlying assumptions; context and changing conditions must be considered.
